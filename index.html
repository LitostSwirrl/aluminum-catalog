Material Philosophy

Form follows
material intelligence

The aluminum extrusion profile is a paradox: endlessly standardized yet infinitely configurable. Pushed through a die at industrial scale, the resulting cross-section becomes a vocabulary unit — a linear module awaiting context.

This catalog maps 100 works across the territories where industrial material meets artistic intention. From Donald Judd's anodized aluminum stacks to El Anatsui's compressed bottle caps, from Sol LeWitt's modular systems to Kengo Kuma's joinery translations — each work interrogates what happens when material is stripped of its original function and re-assembled into new meaning.

The connections between works reveal shared processes, material affinities, and conceptual parallels that cut across traditional art-historical categories. Navigate them through the catalog or explore the force-directed connections map.

Aluminum & Extrusion

Nuage

Ronan & Erwan Bouroullec, 2013

Nuage represents a quietly radical proposition in domestic furniture: a shelving system whose logic derives entirely from the extrusion process itself. Ronan and Erwan Bouroullec designed each module as an extruded aluminum profile that clips together without screws, bolts, or tools, producing an organic, cloud-like aggregation from rigorously industrial means. The name—French for 'cloud'—captures the paradox at the system's heart: hard aluminum yielding soft, amorphous forms. Each module functions as both structure and surface, eliminating the conventional distinction between frame and shelf. The Bouroullecs' achievement lies in recognizing that extrusion's linear constraint could generate three-dimensional freedom when multiplied and rotated. Unlike traditional modular systems that impose grid regularity, Nuage permits asymmetric growth, allowing users to compose arrangements that feel improvised rather than engineered. The system collapses the distance between industrial production and domestic intimacy, proposing that aluminum extrusion—typically hidden inside window frames and curtain walls—can become a visible, even poetic, presence in living spaces.
