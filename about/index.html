Material as
Conceptual Unit

This catalog is a research tool — an investigation into what happens when material is understood not merely as medium, but as a conceptual unit carrying its own logic, history, and generative potential.

The Aluminum Extrusion as Starting Point

The aluminum extrusion profile is deceptively simple: molten aluminum forced through a die to produce a continuous cross-section. The process is industrial, standardized, and infinitely repeatable. Yet the resulting profile — a linear module — carries within it a remarkable tension between constraint and possibility.

The die determines the form. But the form, once liberated from the die, enters a space of radical openness. It can be cut, joined, anodized, bent, stacked, and assembled into configurations that the die never anticipated. The extrusion's identity shifts with each new context: structural member, furniture leg, heat sink, decorative trim, artistic medium.

Material Philosophy

This catalog reflects a specific philosophical position: that material's form and function are contextual attributes, not inherent properties. When an aluminum extrusion is removed from its intended architectural application and placed in a gallery, it doesn't lose its "aluminum-ness" — but it gains new registers of meaning. The industrial profile becomes a readymade. The structural member becomes a module in a visual system. The commodity becomes a conceptual unit.

This process of decontextualization and re-assembly is not unique to aluminum. It operates across materials and scales — from Duchamp's urinal to Serra's weathering steel, from Nakashima's figured walnut to El Anatsui's compressed bottle caps. In each case, the material carries a biography that inflects its artistic present.

Catalog Structure

The 100 works in this catalog are organized across eight categories, each representing a distinct territory where industrial material meets artistic intention. The categories are not mutually exclusive — many works could belong to several — but they provide navigational structure for a fundamentally rhizomatic collection.

More important than the categories are the connections between works. Each entry maps relationships to other works in the catalog: material affinities, conceptual parallels, direct influences, formal similarities, shared processes. These connections can be explored through the interactive force-directed graph on the Connections page.

Methodology

Every entry includes a critical description (200-300 words), a personal relevance note connecting it to the catalog's material philosophy, source citations from established publications, and mapped connections to related works. Sources include museum collection pages, Dezeen, Designboom, Frieze, Artforum, e-flux, academic journals, and gallery publications.
