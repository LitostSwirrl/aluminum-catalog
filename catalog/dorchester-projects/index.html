About this work

Theaster Gates's Dorchester Projects began in 2009 when the artist purchased a vacant two-story building on the 6900 block of South Dorchester Avenue on Chicago's South Side, transforming it into the Archive House, a micro-library and community gathering space. Gates subsequently acquired adjacent abandoned properties and converted them into cultural venues: the Listening House holds eight thousand vinyl LPs salvaged from the closing of Dr. Wax Records in nearby Hyde Park, along with back issues of Ebony and Jet magazines from the Johnson Publishing Company collection. The project operates at the intersection of sculpture, architecture, and social practice, treating abandoned buildings and discarded cultural materials as readymades carrying embedded histories of Black American cultural production. Gates's renovation process deliberately exposes and celebrates the material histories of each structure rather than concealing them behind polished finishes. Reclaimed hardwood floors, salvaged fixtures, and repurposed structural elements become the medium through which neglected neighborhoods are reactivated as sites of cultural exchange. The project fundamentally challenges the gallery system by proposing that art's most significant material transformations can occur within communities rather than institutions.

Catalog Relevance

Extends the concept of material biography beyond the gallery by treating abandoned urban architecture and discarded cultural archives as sculptural materials carrying layered social and racial histories.
