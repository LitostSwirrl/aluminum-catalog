About this work

Tom Sachs's Nutsy's McDonald's is a fully functional, handmade reconstruction of a McDonald's restaurant built from plywood, thermal adhesive, steel hardware, and an arsenal of improvised components including a working grill, fryer, refrigerator, and ice maker. The installation operated as an actual food cart during its exhibition at the Deutsche Guggenheim in Berlin, serving food to visitors while making visible every joint, screw, and imperfection of its bricolaged construction. Sachs deliberately refuses the seamless surfaces of corporate architecture, instead celebrating what he calls the 'truth of materials,' leaving all construction marks, hot glue drips, and Sharpie annotations visible. The work inverts the readymade tradition: rather than presenting a manufactured object as art, Sachs laboriously hand-fabricates a replica of a mass-produced environment, exposing the ideology embedded in corporate design through the transparency of amateur construction. The piece critiques consumer capitalism not through refusal but through obsessive, imperfect replication, revealing how brand identity depends on the suppression of visible labor. Nutsy's McDonald's operates simultaneously as functional architecture, sculptural installation, and performance space, collapsing distinctions between art production, food service, and cultural critique.
