About this work

Shigeru Ban's Paper Tube Structures, beginning in 1986 and reaching their most recognized form in the Paper Church built for earthquake survivors in Kobe, fundamentally challenged architecture's dependence on conventional structural materials. Ban demonstrated that cardboard tubes—typically discarded packaging material—could bear structural loads sufficient for habitable buildings when properly engineered. The Paper Church used fifty-eight paper tubes arranged in an elliptical plan, supporting a tent-like roof membrane that created a luminous, ethereal interior space for a congregation whose original church had been destroyed in the 1995 Great Hanshin earthquake. Ban's innovation was simultaneously technical and ethical: paper tubes are inexpensive, locally available worldwide, recyclable, and require no specialized construction equipment, making them ideal for disaster relief where speed and accessibility are paramount. His subsequent paper tube projects—refugee shelters for the UN, a temporary cathedral in Christchurch, exhibition pavilions across Europe—proved that material innovation need not pursue high-tech solutions but can instead reveal structural potential in materials that industrial society considers worthless. Ban's Pritzker Prize in 2014 recognized how his work reframed architectural material choice as an ethical practice inseparable from questions of social responsibility and environmental stewardship.
