About this work

Ai Weiwei's Sunflower Seeds filled the vast Turbine Hall of Tate Modern with one hundred million individually handcrafted porcelain sunflower seeds, produced over two and a half years by sixteen hundred skilled artisans in the traditional porcelain center of Jingdezhen, China. Each seed was individually molded, fired, and hand-painted to appear indistinguishable from the real thing, yet the sheer accumulation of manual labor embedded in the installation constitutes a staggering counter-argument to industrial mass production. The work references multiple layers of Chinese material and political history: sunflower seeds as a ubiquitous street snack shared among citizens, the sunflower as a Cultural Revolution symbol of loyalty to Mao, and Jingdezhen's centuries-old porcelain tradition now threatened by cheap industrial manufacturing. Initially visitors were invited to walk across the seeds, but health concerns over porcelain dust led Tate to rope off the installation, inadvertently reinforcing the work's themes about the invisible hazards borne by laborers. Ai Weiwei collapses distinctions between craft and industry, individual gesture and collective production, creating a material meditation on what is lost when human labor becomes statistically invisible within systems of mass manufacture.

Catalog Relevance

Confronts industrial mass production with handcraft at industrial scale, using material biography to reveal the human labor and cultural histories concealed within seemingly identical manufactured objects.
