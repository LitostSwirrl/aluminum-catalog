About this work

The Aluminum Group originated from a 1953 commission by Eero Saarinen and Alexander Girard to furnish the Miller House in Columbus, Indiana, with outdoor seating that could withstand weather without sacrificing elegance. Charles and Ray Eames responded with a structural innovation that would reshape office furniture for decades: a die-cast aluminum side frame whose thin profile generates strength through curvature rather than mass, suspending a single panel of fabric or leather in tension between two rails. The design eliminated conventional upholstery padding by making the seat a taut membrane—the sitter's body provides the contour. This move was simultaneously engineering breakthrough and material manifesto: aluminum's high strength-to-weight ratio enabled a frame slender enough to disappear visually while remaining structurally robust. The chair's migration from garden to boardroom revealed how thoroughly it transcended its original context. Its influence on subsequent aluminum seating—from airport lounges to executive suites—established die-cast aluminum as the default language of institutional furniture, a legacy that persists in virtually every contract furniture line produced today.

Catalog Relevance

The Aluminum Group established the paradigm for aluminum as a primary structural material in seating design, demonstrating that die-casting could achieve both engineering efficiency and visual lightness that defined modernist furniture.
