About this work

Tom Price's Meltdown Chair is created by pressing a heated seat-shaped former into a mass of bundled polypropylene rope, causing the synthetic fibers to liquefy on contact and fuse into a solid, functional seat as they cool. No adhesive or additional material is introduced -- the chair is entirely composed of melted and resolidified rope. The process embeds the recognizable silhouette of a conventional armchair within what appears to be an eruption of tangled industrial cordage, creating a visual tension between domesticated comfort and raw material chaos. Price's method is deliberately anti-industrial: where manufacturing seeks to impose uniform order on raw materials, here a blowtorch and brute pressure produce form through controlled destruction. The resulting objects suggest a critical perspective on mass production, simultaneously celebrating and undermining the plastics that define contemporary material culture. Included in permanent collections at SFMOMA and the Denver Art Museum, the Meltdown series establishes Price as a significant figure in the tradition of designers who treat material transformation itself as the primary creative act.

Catalog Relevance

The Meltdown Chair exemplifies direct thermal manipulation of industrial materials into functional form, paralleling processes used in aluminum casting and extrusion where heat transforms rigid stock into fluid, shapeable matter.
