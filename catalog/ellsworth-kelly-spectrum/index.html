About this work

Ellsworth Kelly's Spectrum V stretches thirteen individually painted canvases across nearly twenty-eight feet of wall, each panel a single saturated hue progressing through the visible color spectrum. The work occupies a decisive position between painting and installation: it is simultaneously a sequence of monochrome paintings, a single panoramic composition, and an architectural intervention that transforms whatever wall it inhabits. Kelly arrived at his color practice not through theory but through direct observation—he famously derived his palette from colors encountered in everyday life, from window reflections to shadows on buildings. The Spectrum paintings systematize this empirical approach, presenting color as a physical fact rather than an expressive vehicle. Each panel's hard edge and uniform surface refuse compositional hierarchy, distributing visual weight equally across the sequence. The progression from warm to cool creates a spatial rhythm that pulses between advance and recession, making the flat wall breathe with chromatic depth. Kelly's insistence that color exists as object rather than illusion places Spectrum V at a crucial juncture where postwar painting acknowledged its own physicality, anticipating the shaped canvases and site-specific installations that would follow.

Catalog Relevance

Demonstrates serial color progression as both systematic method and perceptual experience, positioning painting at the boundary between two-dimensional image and three-dimensional object.
