About this work

Antony Gormley's Another Place positions one hundred cast-iron figures along three kilometers of Crosby Beach near Liverpool, each a replica of the artist's own body, standing facing the sea at various distances from the shore. Cast at a foundry in the traditional lost-wax method and weighing approximately 650 kilograms each, the figures are gradually consumed by tidal cycles, accumulating barnacles, rust, and sand. Originally conceived as a temporary installation that traveled to sites in Germany, Norway, and Belgium, the work became permanent at Crosby in 2007 after fierce community advocacy. The iron bodies function as industrial sentinels marking the threshold between land and water, culture and nature, presence and erasure. Gormley exploits cast iron's particular vulnerability to oxidation as a deliberate material strategy: the figures are slowly becoming geological, merging with the beach environment through corrosion. The serial repetition transforms individual portraiture into collective meditation on migration, horizon-gazing, and human vulnerability before elemental forces. Each tidal cycle rewrites the relationship between the industrial figures and their landscape, making the sea itself a collaborator in the work's ongoing material transformation.

Catalog Relevance

Exemplifies how cast iron's oxidation properties become an active sculptural medium, with serial industrial casting placed in dialogue with natural forces of erosion and tidal change.
