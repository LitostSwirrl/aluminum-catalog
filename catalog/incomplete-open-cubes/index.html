About this work

Sol LeWitt's Incomplete Open Cubes is a systematic exploration of every possible variation of an open cube with at least three edges removed, yielding 122 distinct structures displayed together on individual white bases. Each small sculpture is fabricated from painted white aluminum, chosen for its capacity to hold precise right angles at minimal material thickness while maintaining structural integrity across varied cantilevered configurations. The work exemplifies LeWitt's conviction that the idea becomes a machine that makes the art: having established the rule — enumerate all incomplete variations of a cube — the artist simply executes every possibility without aesthetic selection or compositional preference. Yet the visual result transcends its generative logic, as the 122 variations produce unexpectedly diverse formal characters ranging from near-complete cubes missing a single edge to precarious L-shaped fragments barely recognizable as cubic derivatives. LeWitt's aluminum structures occupy a productive threshold between Minimalist objecthood and Conceptual dematerialization: they are rigorously physical objects whose raison d'être is entirely systematic and linguistic. The work reveals how a finite set of modular rules applied to a simple geometric unit generates surprising formal abundance, anticipating computational and algorithmic approaches to art-making by decades.

Catalog Relevance

Demonstrates how aluminum's structural precision enables the physical realization of conceptual rule-based systems, bridging Minimalist material logic with Conceptual art's emphasis on permutation and exhaustive enumeration.
