About this work

Doris Salcedo's Shibboleth opened a 167-meter crack through the concrete floor of Tate Modern's Turbine Hall, beginning as a hairline fissure at the entrance and widening to a chasm nearly sixty centimeters deep, revealing embedded wire mesh fencing within the ruptured concrete. The work took Salcedo's team months of covert engineering to realize, carefully fracturing the floor to simulate a seismic event that appeared to split the building's industrial foundation. The title references the biblical term for a linguistic test used to identify and exclude outsiders — a mechanism of division encoded in speech. Salcedo's crack makes literal the metaphor of social fracture, embedding the infrastructure of exclusion (wire fencing associated with borders and detention) within the institutional fabric of a major Western museum. The concrete floor, typically the invisible industrial substrate supporting art's elevated discourse, becomes the artwork itself through its violation. By wounding rather than adding to the architecture, Salcedo inverted the monumental tradition of Turbine Hall commissions, proposing that absence and rupture can carry greater material and political weight than sculptural presence. The repaired scar remains visible in the floor, a permanent index of the intervention.

Catalog Relevance

Transforms industrial concrete infrastructure from passive architectural substrate into active political material, using the crack as both literal engineering challenge and metaphor for social divisions embedded within institutional structures.
