About this work

Monika Sosnowska's Habitat series subjects heavy steel construction beams -- the skeletal vocabulary of modern architecture -- to violent physical intervention, crushing, twisting, and compressing them into new configurations that retain the memory of their original structural purpose while rendering it absurd. Drawing inspiration from the hyperboloid steel towers of Russian engineer Vladimir Shukhov and informed by a section collapse of Moscow's Shabolovka Tower from metal fatigue, the works present a choreographed destruction in which industrial materials undergo what might be called sculptural stress testing. The beams are fabricated at one-to-one scale using the same materials as their architectural referents, then deformed under Sosnowska's direction using hydraulic presses, cranes, and chains. Finished with pristine coats of black paint, the resulting forms possess a paradoxical elegance: smooth curves, flowing kinks, and lyrical contortions that belie their origin in brute mechanical force. The work channels Poland's fraught relationship with Soviet-era modernist architecture, transforming the rigid geometries of ideological construction into objects that express the entropy, collapse, and stubborn persistence of built environments shaped by political systems no longer in power.

Catalog Relevance

Sosnowska's physical manipulation of structural steel beams into sculptural form directly parallels processes of metal deformation central to aluminum fabrication, where industrial material is bent, pressed, and reformed to serve new purposes.
