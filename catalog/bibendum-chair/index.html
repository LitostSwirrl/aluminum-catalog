About this work

The Bibendum Chair, named for its resemblance to the Michelin tire mascot, is Eileen Gray's most iconic furniture design and one of the earliest examples of tubular steel used in domestic seating. Designed in 1926, the same period when Marcel Breuer was developing his Wassily Chair at the Bauhaus, Gray's approach to the new material was characteristically independent: where Breuer exposed the steel tube as an aesthetic statement of industrial modernity, Gray upholstered her chair in generous padded leather rings that soften and feminize the industrial frame. The semi-circular chromed steel base provides a cantilevered spring, while three stacked leather cushion rings create a voluptuous, embracing form that prioritizes bodily comfort over visual austerity. Gray's design reveals a fundamentally different understanding of modernism than her male contemporaries pursued: technology serves sensory pleasure rather than ideological demonstration. The Bibendum Chair proves that tubular steel need not produce the angular, hard-edged furniture associated with Bauhaus orthodoxy. Gray's long exclusion from canonical design histories, despite working contemporaneously with Breuer, Mies, and Le Corbusier, reflects the gendered biases that shaped modernism's self-narration throughout the twentieth century.

Catalog Relevance

Demonstrates an alternative approach to tubular metal furniture design that prioritizes comfort and sensuality over industrial expression, revealing how the same material technology that produced austere modernist icons could equally generate voluptuous, body-centered forms when freed from ideological constraints.
