About this work

Chair_ONE by Konstantin Grcic represents a radical rethinking of the seated object through aluminum die-casting. The chair's form emerges from a triangulated structural logic — each face of its shell is a flat plane, creating a faceted, almost crystalline geometry that recalls geodesic structures more than conventional furniture. Grcic worked extensively with engineering software typically used in automotive and aerospace design, treating the chair as a structural problem rather than a formal one. The result is a seat that appears impossibly thin and open, yet achieves remarkable strength through its triangulated surface. The aluminum is not decorative here; it is the argument. The material's ability to be cast into complex thin-walled geometries allows a form that would be impossible in wood, impractical in steel, and structurally unsound in plastic at this thickness. Chair_ONE makes visible the logic of aluminum as a structural material — the same logic that governs extrusion profiles, where geometry compensates for material thinness. The chair has become an icon of early 21st-century design precisely because it refuses comfort as a primary value, instead proposing that a chair can be primarily a structural and material proposition.

Catalog Relevance

Chair_ONE demonstrates how aluminum's structural logic — thin walls, triangulated geometry — can generate form. The same principle governs extrusion profiles where cross-sectional geometry creates strength from minimal material.
