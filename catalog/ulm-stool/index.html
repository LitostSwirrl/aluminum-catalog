About this work

The Ulm Stool represents Max Bill's conviction that design education and democratic access were inseparable pursuits. Created for the Hochschule für Gestaltung in Ulm, the school Bill co-founded as a successor to the Bauhaus, this deceptively simple object served simultaneously as seat, shelf, side table, and carrying device. Its construction from three pieces of beech—two flat planes and a cylindrical crossbar—reduces furniture to an irreducible logic of material and function. The stool rejected postwar consumer culture's appetite for decorative excess, proposing instead that an object's beauty emerges from its structural honesty. Bill, trained as both architect and artist under the Bauhaus masters, embodied the Swiss Concrete Art movement's insistence on mathematical clarity. The Ulm Stool became the physical manifesto of his educational philosophy: students carried them between classrooms, stacked them as impromptu shelving, and lived with them as proof that rigorous formal thinking could produce objects of genuine utility. Its enduring production testifies to a design so resolved that sixty years of furniture innovation have found nothing to add or subtract.
