About this work

The GC Prostho Museum Research Center represents Kengo Kuma's most radical investigation into traditional Japanese joinery as a contemporary structural principle. The building's facade and structural lattice are assembled entirely from cypress wood sticks joined using the cidori technique, a traditional toy-making method where interlocking wooden pieces hold together without nails, screws, or adhesive. Each stick is notched at precise intervals, allowing perpendicular members to slot together in a three-dimensional grid that gains strength through mutual compression. The result is a building that appears woven rather than constructed, its lattice simultaneously serving as structure, envelope, and ornament. Kuma's genius lies in scaling an intimate craft technique to architectural dimensions, demonstrating that pre-industrial joining methods can achieve the performance demands of modern building codes. The lattice filters light into the interior galleries with a warmth impossible in steel or concrete construction. The project challenges the assumption that advanced architecture requires advanced materials, proposing instead that sophistication resides in the intelligence of connections rather than the properties of components.

Catalog Relevance

Demonstrates how traditional joining systems achieve structural complexity without industrial fasteners, offering a counterpoint to aluminum extrusion's reliance on mechanical connections and presenting craft knowledge as a viable alternative to material engineering.
