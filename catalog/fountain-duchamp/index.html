About this work

Marcel Duchamp's Fountain is the fracture point of modern art—the moment when the manufactured object entered the gallery not as subject but as artwork itself. Submitted to the 1917 Society of Independent Artists exhibition under the pseudonym R. Mutt, the inverted Bedfordshire-type urinal was rejected despite the society's open-submission policy, provoking exactly the institutional crisis Duchamp intended. The gesture was not nihilistic but philosophical: by selecting, rotating, signing, and titling a mass-produced porcelain fixture, Duchamp demonstrated that artistic value resides in the act of designation rather than in material transformation. The original was lost almost immediately, likely discarded, but Duchamp authorized replicas in the 1960s, adding another layer of irony—the readymade becoming a carefully crafted reproduction. Fountain's legacy is essentially the entire trajectory of conceptual art, from Warhol's Brillo Boxes to Koons's vacuum cleaners to every subsequent artist who has placed an industrial object in a white cube. Its radicalism has been so thoroughly absorbed that it risks seeming quaint, yet no single work has done more to dismantle the boundary between manufactured commodity and aesthetic experience.

Catalog Relevance

Fountain establishes the foundational logic for every artwork in this catalog that recontextualizes industrial materials—without Duchamp's readymade, the idea of aluminum extrusion or fluorescent tubes as art would lack its conceptual framework.
