About this work

Subodh Gupta's 'Very Hungry God' is a monumental skull constructed from hundreds of stainless steel pots, pans, plates, and kitchen utensils welded together into a gleaming, totemic form. First exhibited hanging from the façade of the Musée de la Monnaie during Paris's Nuit Blanche festival, the work transforms the humble thali plate and cooking vessel—ubiquitous objects of Indian domestic life—into a vanitas symbol of global proportions. The skull, art history's most enduring memento mori, becomes a vessel for collective memory: each utensil carries the residue of meals prepared, families fed, daily rituals performed. Gupta, sometimes called the 'Damien Hirst of Delhi,' operates in the tradition of the readymade but inflects it with specifically postcolonial concerns about material culture, migration, and the sacred status of food in Indian society. The stainless steel surface creates a dazzling mirror effect, reflecting viewers back at themselves while confronting them with mortality. The work's sheer scale transforms domestic objects into architectural spectacle, challenging hierarchies between the kitchen and the museum.

Catalog Relevance

Illustrates how everyday metal objects can be recontextualized through accumulation and welding to address cultural identity, consumption, and the material biography of domestic life.
