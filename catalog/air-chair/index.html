About this work

Jasper Morrison's 'Air-Chair' for Magis was one of the first chairs produced using gas-injection molding technology, a process that injects nitrogen gas into molten polypropylene to create hollow internal channels that reduce material usage while maintaining structural rigidity. The result is an extraordinarily lightweight single-piece chair—stackable, weather-resistant, and economical—that achieves its elegance through process innovation rather than formal extravagance. Morrison's design philosophy of 'Super Normal' is fully realized here: the chair is so restrained, so devoid of stylistic gesture, that it approaches invisibility. Yet this apparent simplicity masks sophisticated engineering; the gas channels function as internal ribbing, creating structural performance that solid plastic could not achieve at comparable weight. The Air-Chair represents a paradigm where the manufacturing process itself becomes the primary design decision, collapsing the distinction between engineering and aesthetics. Its commercial success proved that advanced industrial processes could serve everyday objects at democratic price points, establishing a template for process-driven furniture design that continues to influence the industry.

Catalog Relevance

Exemplifies how advanced industrial molding processes can define furniture form, paralleling aluminum extrusion's logic of letting manufacturing technology determine aesthetic outcomes.
