About this work

Pierre Jeanneret's Chandigarh Chair stands as one of the most consequential furniture designs of the twentieth century, born from the unprecedented project of building an entire modernist city in post-independence India. The chair's signature compass-leg structure, forming a distinctive V-shape, achieves remarkable structural efficiency while maintaining visual lightness. Jeanneret designed it specifically for the administrative buildings of Chandigarh, Le Corbusier's planned capital of Punjab, adapting European modernist principles to local materials and craft traditions. The teak frame and hand-woven cane seat reflect a pragmatic engagement with Indian woodworking and weaving techniques, creating a hybrid object that is neither purely Western nor purely Indian. For decades these chairs were considered mere government furniture, left to deteriorate in offices and streets. Their rediscovery and subsequent elevation to blue-chip collectible status raises pointed questions about how colonial modernism is valued, fetishized, and decontextualized. The chair's journey from civic object to auction commodity encapsulates broader tensions between design history and market forces.
