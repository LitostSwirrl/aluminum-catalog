About this work

Charlotte Perriand designed the Méribel Table for the ski resort of Méribel-les-Allues in the French Alps, where she served as architectural advisor from 1946. The table embodies her mature synthesis of two seemingly opposed commitments: the machine-age rationalism she absorbed during her years with Le Corbusier, and the deep respect for natural materials and craft she developed through her engagement with Japanese woodworking tradition. The oak top, with its visible grain and organic warmth, rests on legs that combine aluminum and steel in a structural configuration borrowed from architecture rather than cabinetmaking. Perriand's use of aluminum here is pragmatic and poetic simultaneously—its corrosion resistance suits Alpine conditions, while its industrial character creates a deliberate tension with the handworked wood above. The table refuses to choose between rusticity and modernism, insisting that both can coexist without irony or pastiche. This hybrid sensibility anticipated by decades the current design discourse around material honesty and contextual sensitivity. Cassina's continued production preserves Perriand's vision of furniture as architecture at intimate scale, where every joint articulates a relationship between industrial capability and human dwelling.

Catalog Relevance

The Méribel Table demonstrates aluminum's capacity to mediate between industrial modernity and craft tradition in furniture, embodying a hybrid material philosophy that remains central to contemporary design discourse.
