About this work

Tom Dixon's 'S Chair' emerged from his early experiments with salvage welding in London's creative underground of the 1980s. The chair's continuous S-curve frame, welded from steel rod, supports a woven wicker or rush seat that softens the industrial skeleton with organic texture. Dixon, a self-taught designer who learned to weld by making sculptures from scrap metal, brought a raw, improvisational energy to furniture that contrasted sharply with the polished Italian design establishment. When Cappellini put the S Chair into production in 1991, it signaled a new direction for British design—one rooted in material experimentation and maker culture rather than academic formalism. The sinuous steel line performs structurally while creating a dynamic visual profile that shifts dramatically depending on viewing angle. The marriage of industrial welded rod with handwoven natural fiber creates a productive tension between machine-age and craft traditions, suggesting that these categories need not be oppositional. The S Chair launched Dixon's career and remains an icon of the creative salvage movement's transition into mainstream design production.
