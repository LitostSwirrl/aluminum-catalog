About this work

The Angel Chair represents the apex of Wendell Castle's stack-lamination technique, a method he pioneered in the 1960s that fundamentally redefined the relationship between wood furniture and sculpture. Rather than joining discrete components through traditional joinery, Castle glued together thick planks of mahogany into a massive block, then carved the chair from this monolithic mass as a sculptor would approach stone. The Angel Chair emerges from its block with sweeping, biomorphic curves that seem to defy wood's natural grain structure, its flowing back rising into wing-like extensions that give the piece its name. The surface is carved and sanded to a seamless continuity that conceals the lamination lines, producing an object that appears grown rather than made. Castle's approach was deliberately transgressive within the craft world: by treating wood as sculptural volume rather than structural member, he collapsed the hierarchy that separated fine art from applied craft. The Angel Chair sits with equal authority in a living room or a museum gallery, refusing the distinction between functional object and autonomous artwork that had organized design discourse since the Bauhaus.

Catalog Relevance

Castle's stack-lamination technique parallels the logic of aluminum extrusion in reverse: where extrusion forces material through a die to create profile, Castle builds up material mass then subtracts form, both methods producing continuous shapes impossible through conventional assembly.
