About this work

David Adjaye's Washington Skeleton Chair strips a chair down to its essential structural diagram, exposing the network of forces that hold a seated body. The die-cast aluminum frame reveals every load path and stress point, transforming what is normally hidden inside upholstery into an explicitly legible exoskeleton. Adjaye, primarily known as an architect, approached the chair as a building in miniature, applying architectural thinking about structure, material efficiency, and spatial enclosure to a domestic object. The aluminum casting process allowed him to achieve geometries impossible in bent tube or welded fabrication, creating organic branching forms that recall both Gothic tracery and biological bone structure. The chair explicitly references the Eames Aluminum Group, acknowledging aluminum's central role in twentieth-century furniture innovation while pushing the material into new formal territory through contemporary casting technology. Each chair is produced in a single die-cast operation, meaning the entire frame emerges from one pour, eliminating joints and fasteners. This manufacturing directness gives the object an integrity that mirrors Adjaye's architectural commitment to honest material expression.

Catalog Relevance

Directly engages aluminum as primary medium, connecting die-casting technology to architectural structural logic and extending the Eames legacy of aluminum furniture into contemporary practice.
