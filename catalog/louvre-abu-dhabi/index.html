About this work

Jean Nouvel's Louvre Abu Dhabi centers on a 180-meter dome composed of eight interlocking layers -- four outer layers clad in stainless steel and four inner layers clad in aluminum -- forming a geometric constellation of 7,850 stars of varying sizes and angles. The structure weighs approximately 7,500 tonnes, comparable to the Eiffel Tower, yet appears to float above the museum complex, supported by only four piers hidden within the buildings below. Nouvel describes the filtered illumination passing through this perforated canopy as a 'rain of light,' a contemporary reinterpretation of the dappled shade created by interwoven palm fronds in traditional Arab souks. The layered pattern permits only 1.8 percent of direct sunlight to penetrate at any given moment, creating constantly shifting patterns across the galleries and walkways beneath. The dome eliminates 98 percent of solar heat gain, functioning simultaneously as environmental screen, cultural symbol, and sculptural form -- collapsing the distinctions between engineering, ornament, and shelter into a single monumental gesture.

Catalog Relevance

The dome's aluminum-clad lattice layers represent one of the most ambitious applications of aluminum in contemporary architecture, using the material's lightness and reflectivity to create monumental structural poetry from geometric repetition.
