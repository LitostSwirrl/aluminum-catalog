About this work

Dan Flavin's Monument for V. Tatlin is a series of approximately fifty works produced between 1964 and 1990, each composed of standard commercially available fluorescent light tubes arranged in vertical configurations that evoke—without literally depicting—Vladimir Tatlin's unrealized Monument to the Third International. The homage is deliberately ironic: where Tatlin envisioned a colossal spiraling tower of steel and glass housing the Soviet government, Flavin offers humble hardware-store fixtures plugged into wall outlets. The substitution of revolutionary ambition with everyday industrial readymades is both affectionate and critical. Flavin's fluorescent tubes are not sculpted or modified; they are purchased, arranged, and illuminated. The art lies in composition, color temperature, and the way light dissolves the boundary between object and environment, flooding surrounding walls with colored ambient glow. Each Monument varies in tube count, color, and configuration, yet all maintain the upward vertical thrust that echoes Tatlin's spiral. Flavin insisted his works were "situations" rather than sculptures, emphasizing that the experience included the entire illuminated space, not just the fixtures themselves.

Catalog Relevance

Flavin's use of unmodified industrial lighting fixtures directly parallels the use of stock aluminum extrusions in art and design—both demonstrate that commercial industrial products, deployed with intention, can achieve profound aesthetic and conceptual resonance.
