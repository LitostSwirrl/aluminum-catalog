About this work

Oscar Tuazon's Building inserts raw architectural elements directly into the gallery space, creating structures that hover between functional architecture and sculptural proposition. Massive concrete slabs rest on frameworks of unfinished pine beams, roughly chain-sawed to size and bolted together with exposed steel hardware, while the assembled forms achieve equilibrium through mutually counterbalancing forces rather than conventional engineering. Tuazon, who studied at Deep Springs College and the Whitney Independent Study Program, draws on direct experience with vernacular construction to produce works that foreground architecture's ancillary components, including stairways, doorframes, and structural supports that are typically concealed behind finished surfaces. The sculptures operate as full-scale prototypes that question how buildings might be constructed differently, proposing an architecture of open modification rather than fixed design. By introducing raw construction materials and unfinished structural logic into institutional gallery spaces, Tuazon creates a productive tension between the white cube's controlled environment and the unruly materiality of actual building practice. The work challenges visitors to physically navigate around and through its imposing forms, restoring a bodily encounter with architectural scale that polished gallery spaces typically suppress.
