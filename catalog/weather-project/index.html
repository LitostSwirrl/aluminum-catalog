About this work

Olafur Eliasson's The Weather Project transformed Tate Modern's cavernous Turbine Hall into a space of collective wonder, installing an enormous artificial sun at the far end of the hall while lining the ceiling with mirrors that doubled the apparent height of the space. Hundreds of mono-frequency sodium lamps—the same type used in street lighting—produced a warm monochromatic glow that reduced all color to shades of yellow and black, while haze machines filled the hall with a fine mist that dissolved architectural edges into atmospheric gradients. The mirror ceiling proved transformative: visitors lay on the floor gazing upward at their own reflections, spontaneously forming patterns and messages with their bodies. Over two million people visited during its five-month run, making it one of the most attended installations in history. Eliasson's achievement was not merely spectacular but epistemological—by revealing the mechanisms of his illusion (the semicircular sun, the visible lamps, the mirror seams), he invited viewers to oscillate between immersive experience and critical awareness. The work demonstrated that contemporary art could create genuinely public, physically transformative encounters while maintaining intellectual rigor about the constructed nature of perception.
