About this work

Nendo's 'Manga Chairs' is a collection of 50 chairs, each constructed from thin steel rod with powder coating, featuring exaggerated cartoon-like extensions that sprout from the basic chair silhouette. These appendages—drips, splashes, motion lines, speech bubbles, and speed marks—borrow the visual grammar of manga comics and translate two-dimensional graphic conventions into three-dimensional functional objects. Oki Sato's approach treats the chair as a baseline canvas, a universally recognizable archetype onto which narrative embellishments can be grafted. The wireframe construction strips the chair to its essential linear skeleton, making the boundary between structure and ornament ambiguous. Each variation reads as a single visual gag, yet collectively they form a systematic taxonomy of graphic expression rendered in steel. The project sits at the intersection of industrial design and visual storytelling, questioning whether furniture can communicate narrative content. By deploying humor and graphic language, Nendo challenges the solemnity of design discourse while maintaining rigorous formal consistency across the entire series.

Catalog Relevance

Explores how industrial steel rod construction can bridge graphic communication and functional furniture, using systematic variation to create a modular vocabulary of chair forms.
