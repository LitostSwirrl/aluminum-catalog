About this work

Felix Gonzalez-Torres's '"Untitled" (Portrait of Ross in L.A.)' consists of a pile of individually wrapped candies whose ideal weight of 175 pounds corresponds to the healthy body weight of the artist's partner, Ross Laycock, who died of AIDS-related complications in January 1991. Visitors are invited to take a piece of candy, gradually diminishing the pile -- a participatory enactment of the body's wasting that transforms each viewer into an unwitting agent of loss. The work is designated as having an 'endless supply,' meaning the pile is periodically replenished, creating a cycle of depletion and renewal that refuses the finality of death while acknowledging its inexorability. Gonzalez-Torres's genius lies in his use of Minimalism's formal vocabulary -- the floor-based accumulation of identical units, the precise specification of quantity -- to smuggle profound emotional and political content into an art-world language that had declared such content irrelevant. The colored cellophane wrappers introduce a sweetness and visual generosity that makes the work's devastating subject bearable, even pleasurable, implicating the viewer's desire in the very consumption that the piece mourns.

Catalog Relevance

The work's use of precisely weighed industrial multiples to encode biographical narrative exemplifies how manufactured materials can carry deeply personal meaning through quantity, weight, and the process of material diminishment.
