About this work

In Hale is a series of hybrid objects in which Ben Storms has molten glass blown directly into and around rigid aluminum structural frames, creating pieces where two fundamentally different materials meet at the moment of formation. The glassblower inflates the molten glass bubble so that it encounters the aluminum skeleton, pressing against and enveloping the metal members while still in its viscous state. As the glass cools, it captures the aluminum in an embrace that is both structural and sensual, the transparent material revealing the metal frame imprisoned within its body. The result is a series of tables, vessels, and sculptural objects that appear to breathe, the glass swelling organically around the geometric aluminum like a living organism consuming an inorganic host. Storms's technique makes visible the thermal drama of making: the glass remembers its liquid state through its bulging, biomorphic forms, while the aluminum retains its cold geometric precision. The work explores what happens when materials with radically different thermal behaviors, melting points, and structural logics are forced into intimate contact during the vulnerable moment of formation, producing objects that are neither glass nor metal but an irreversible hybrid of both.

Catalog Relevance

Directly engages aluminum as a structural armature for glass, exploring how the metal's thermal stability and geometric precision interact with molten glass's fluid unpredictability, creating hybrid objects that reveal both materials' properties through their encounter.
